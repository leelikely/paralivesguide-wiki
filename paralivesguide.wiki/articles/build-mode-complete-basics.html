Build Mode: The Complete Basics

Walls, roofs, windows, floors — every building tool in Paralives explained step by step, from your first empty lot to a fully decorated home.

📅 Published: January 20, 2026·🔄 Updated: January 20, 2026

Overview

Let me tell you something I wish someone had told me before I opened Paralives Build Mode for the first time: this is not The Sims. Yes, it looks familiar on the surface — walls, furniture, roofing — but the moment you start clicking around, you realize the tools work completely differently. And honestly? That's a good thing.

Paralives Build Mode was designed from scratch, and it shows. The developers clearly looked at what frustrated players about other building systems and said, "Yeah, let's not do that." The result is a toolset that feels intuitive once you understand it, but can also throw you for a loop if you go in expecting a clone of something else.

This guide covers everything you need to build your first house — from laying down your first wall to placing the finishing decorative touches. I'll walk you through each tool, explain what it does, and point out the features that aren't immediately obvious (there are a few hidden gems).

💡 Tip: Before you start building, make sure you have enough in-game currency. Building costs money, and nothing kills the creative flow faster than running out of funds halfway through your dream kitchen. Start small if you're on a budget — you can always expand later.

Getting Into Build Mode

You enter Build Mode by clicking the hammer icon in the bottom-right corner of the screen — or by pressing B on your keyboard (which you'll be doing approximately eight hundred times per play session once you get addicted to renovating).

The interface is split into a few main areas:

  • Tool Palette — Left side of the screen. This is where you pick what you're doing: building walls, placing furniture, landscaping, etc.
  • Catalog — Bottom panel. Shows available items for the selected tool, sorted by category and style.
  • Info Bar — Top of the screen. Shows your current lot value, room dimensions as you draw, and the ever-important Undo button.
  • Grid Overlay — The dotted lines on your lot. Toggle with G if they're distracting you.

One thing I love: the catalog has a style filter that lets you show only Modern, Rustic, Vintage, or Eclectic pieces. If you're someone who gets overwhelmed by too many options (raises hand), this is a lifesaver.

Walls & Rooms

Building starts with walls. Click the Wall Tool (or press W), then click and drag on your lot to draw. Paralives uses a click-and-drag system rather than the traditional "click start, click end" method — you literally draw walls the way you'd draw a line in a paint program. It's faster once you get used to it.

Wall Heights

You can set walls to three heights: Low, Medium, and Tall. Low walls are great for cozy cottages or minimalist modern builds. Tall walls make rooms feel grand and airy — perfect for entryways, great rooms, or anywhere you want to show off tall windows. You can mix wall heights on the same floor, which is genuinely unique and lets you create split-level effects without complicated tricks.

Curved Walls

Here's the feature that made my jaw drop the first time I used it. While drawing a wall, hold Shift and drag sideways — the wall curves. You can control the curve radius in real time. Want a circular tower? Done. A gently curved living room wall with floor-to-ceiling windows? Absolutely. This single feature opens up architectural possibilities that used to require mods in other games.

Rooms

When you draw a closed shape with walls, the game automatically recognizes it as a room. You'll see a faint highlight and the room's dimensions pop up in the info bar. Click inside the room to select it — from there you can change the floor type, wall covering, ceiling, and (if it's on the ground floor) add a foundation.

Floors & Roofs

Once your walls are up, you need floors and a roof. Paralives handles these separately from walls, which took me a bit to wrap my head around.

Floors

The Floor Tool lets you paint surfaces — wood, tile, stone, carpet, you name it. You apply it per-room or per-level. One neat trick: you can paint partial floors by holding Ctrl and selecting specific tiles. This is great for creating area rugs, kitchen tile patterns, or transition strips between rooms.

Roofs

Roofs in Paralives are auto-generated but fully adjustable. Click "Add Roof" and the game will place a basic gable roof on your structure. From there you can change the roof type (gable, hip, flat, gambrel, and a few more), adjust the pitch by dragging, and add dormers. The roof tool also supports overhangs — drag the eaves out for a craftsman look, or tuck them in for a modern flat-roof aesthetic.

If you're building a multi-story house, you can place different roof types on different sections. Add a gable over the main house and a flat roof over the garage extension — the game handles the intersection cleanly without leaving weird gaps (which was the bane of my existence in other builders).

Doors & Windows

Placement is straightforward: pick from the catalog, click on a wall, done. But there are a few things worth knowing:

  • Doors and windows snap to the grid by default. Hold Alt for free placement — great for off-center windows or unconventional layouts.
  • Front doors are special. The game designates the first exterior door you place as the "front door." This is where visitors knock and where mail gets delivered. You can change it later by right-clicking any exterior door and selecting "Set as Front Door."
  • Window height is adjustable. After placing a window, you can drag it up or down the wall. Want high clerestory windows for privacy? Go for it.
  • Curtains and blinds are separate items that auto-attach to windows when placed nearby. They'll resize to match the window width, which is a small touch that saves so much fiddling.

Furniture & Decor

This is where things get fun. The furniture catalog is extensive — and I mean extensive. You've got everything from beds and sofas to potted plants and wall art. A few highlights:

Resizable Objects

Select any furniture item and press [ or ] to shrink or enlarge it. Want an oversized statement painting? A tiny side table that fits in a narrow hallway? The resize tool handles it. Just keep an eye on functionality — resize a dining table too small and Paras won't be able to eat at it, even if it looks cute.

Color Swatches & Materials

Almost every item has multiple color options, and some have material variants (wood vs. metal vs. fabric). You can also use the Color Picker tool to sample a color from one item and apply it to another — perfect for matching wood tones across different furniture pieces.

The Clutter Rule

Here's a personal tip: clutter makes a house feel lived-in. Books on coffee tables, dishes by the sink, a jacket thrown over a chair — these tiny details are what turn a "nice build" into a "wow, I want to live there" build. Paralives has a dedicated Clutter category in the catalog. Use it liberally.

Advanced Features Worth Knowing

Foundations & Decks

Foundations raise your entire house off the ground. You set the height in the Room Properties panel. Decks work similarly but are placed as separate "rooms" without walls. You can connect a deck to the house and add stairs down to the yard. The stair tool auto-adjusts based on the height difference, which is one less thing to worry about.

Basements

Yes, basements! Select the Basement Tool and draw underground rooms the same way you draw above-ground ones. You'll need stairs to access them, obviously. Lighting is dimmer underground by default, so plan for plenty of light fixtures.

Multi-Story Building

Once your ground floor is done, click "Add Level" to build a second story. The game shows a ghosted outline of the floor below so you can align walls. Stairs connect the levels — and there are multiple stair styles, from straight runs to L-shaped and spiral staircases.

The Undo Button Is Your Best Friend

Ctrl+Z. Memorize it. Love it. Paralives has a generous undo history, so don't be afraid to experiment. Try that weird roof shape, place that bold wallpaper — if it doesn't work, just undo.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Forgetting to check lot boundaries. Your lot has edges. Build too close to them and you won't have space for a yard, driveway, or expansions. Zoom out and check the lot outline before you commit.
  2. Ignoring lighting. A beautifully furnished room looks terrible in the dark. Place light fixtures in every room — ceiling lights, floor lamps, wall sconces. Mix light sources for depth.
  3. Building too big too fast. I know the empty lot is tempting. But a massive house takes forever to furnish and costs a fortune. Start with 2-3 rooms and expand as your household grows.
  4. Not using the style filter. The furniture catalog is huge. Filtering by style cuts the options down to something manageable and helps keep your build visually cohesive.
  5. Skipping landscaping. A house with no plants, no path, no outdoor details looks plopped-down and unnatural. Even a few bushes and a walkway make a huge difference.

Ready to Build?

That covers the fundamentals! Once you're comfortable with the basics, check out our guides on furniture arrangement, outdoor spaces, and advanced roofing techniques (all coming soon). For now, grab an empty lot and start experimenting — the best way to learn Build Mode is to build stuff.
